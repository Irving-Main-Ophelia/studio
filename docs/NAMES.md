# Project Name — Resolved: **Stockhausen**

> **Final decision (May 13, 2026):** The project is named **Stockhausen** in homage to **Karlheinz Stockhausen** (1928–2007), the towering figure of post-war electronic and electroacoustic composition.
>
> This document is kept for historical reference — the alternatives considered and the reasoning behind the final choice.

---

## Track A — Terms where music and electronics intersect (cool / technical / particular)

| # | Candidate | What it means / why it works |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Oscilla** | An oscillator is the heart of both an electronic synth and an acoustic vibrating string. Feminine, sonorous, evocative. |
| 2 | **Klangwerk** | German *"sound-work"*. Kraftwerk-energy: tools, machines, and music. Iconic in electronic-music history. |
| 3 | **Phasor** | A phasor is a vector representing a sinusoid's amplitude and phase — the math both electrical engineers and acousticians live in. Sharp, modern. |
| 4 | **Resonata** | *Resonance* + *sonata*. Names the moment a physical body or a piece of software *sings*. |
| 5 | **Cadenza** | The classical-music name for an improvised solo passage. Implies virtuosity and agency — what we hand the composer. |
| 6 | **Tessera** | Plural *tessera* — the tiles of a mosaic. Also a near-cousin of *tessitura* (the comfortable register of a voice/instrument). Evokes composition as assembly. |
| 7 | **Aether** | The classical fifth element; also the historic name for a hypothetical wave-carrying medium. Romantic and technical at once. |
| 8 | **Diapason** | Greek *"through all"* — the full musical compass, also the tuning fork's reference pitch. Old, deep, perfect. |
| 9 | **Modus** | Latin *"manner / way / mode"*. Mode is the heart of musical character (radif, maqam, rāga, church modes). |
| 10 | **Polyphon** | From *polyphony*, the art of many independent voices. Also a 19th-century mechanical-music machine. |
| 11 | **Spectrum** | Where audio engineers live: the frequency-domain picture of sound. Confident, technical, broad. |
| 12 | **Voltus** | Coined from *voltage* + Latin *vultus* (countenance, face). Has a futurist clang. |
| 13 | **Synapse** | The point where one neuron meets another — the metaphor for human+AI co-composition. |
| 14 | **Lattice** | A periodic structure (physics, crystallography, music theory's neo-Riemannian *Tonnetz*). Connects engineering and harmony. |
| 15 | **Quanta** | The smallest unit. In music, a note; in audio, a sample; in physics, a quantum. Concise, modern. |
| 16 | **Caprice** | A free-spirited piece form (Paganini's caprices, Mendelssohn's, Liszt's). Implies agility and surprise. |
| 17 | **Sympathon** | Coined: *sympathetic resonance* + a Greek suffix. Carries the idea of strings vibrating in sympathy (sitar, qanun, tanpura). |
| 18 | **Sonance** | Old-English *sounding*. Plain, clean, musical. |
| 19 | **Helix** | The shape that DNA, sound waves, and spiral notebooks share. Composition as growth. |
| 20 | **Continuum** | The Haken Continuum is one of the most expressive electronic instruments ever built; *continuum* also names the unbroken pitch space behind notation. |

**My top three picks from Track A:** *Oscilla*, *Klangwerk*, *Resonata*.

## Track B — Pioneer composers of electronic / electroacoustic music

Each of these people was a serious classical composer who fought to bring electronics into the concert hall.

| # | Candidate | Who they were |
|---|---|---|
| 1 | **Theremin** | *Léon Theremin* (1896–1993) — invented the theremin (1920), one of the first electronic instruments. The patron saint of music + electronics. |
| 2 | **Stockhausen** | *Karlheinz Stockhausen* (1928–2007) — the towering figure of post-war electronic composition (*Gesang der Jünglinge*, *Studie I/II*). |
| 3 | **Varèse** | *Edgard Varèse* (1883–1965) — *Poème électronique* (1958); the prophet who insisted on the future of electronic sound. |
| 4 | **Schaeffer** | *Pierre Schaeffer* (1910–1995) — founded *musique concrète*; first to treat recorded sound as compositional material. |
| 5 | **Xenakis** | *Iannis Xenakis* (1922–2001) — architect, composer, mathematician. Used stochastic processes and computers (the UPIC system). Most relevant to Stockhausen's spirit. |
| 6 | **Babbitt** | *Milton Babbitt* (1916–2011) — serialist; worked on the RCA Mark II synthesizer at Columbia-Princeton. |
| 7 | **Subotnick** | *Morton Subotnick* (b. 1933) — *Silver Apples of the Moon* (1967) was the first all-electronic album commissioned by a record label. Co-developer of the Buchla. |
| 8 | **Carlos** | *Wendy Carlos* (b. 1939) — *Switched-On Bach* (1968) brought classical music + Moog to mainstream ears. |
| 9 | **Oliveros** | *Pauline Oliveros* (1932–2016) — tape music, *Deep Listening*; one of the most original sonic minds of the 20th century. |
| 10 | **Boulez** | *Pierre Boulez* (1925–2016) — founded IRCAM in Paris, the institute where serious electronic music research lives. |
| 11 | **Berio** | *Luciano Berio* (1925–2003) — founded the *Studio di fonologia* in Milan; *Thema (Omaggio a Joyce)*. |
| 12 | **Maderna** | *Bruno Maderna* (1920–1973) — co-founded the Milan studio; foundational to Italian electronic composition. |
| 13 | **Eimert** | *Herbert Eimert* (1897–1972) — founded the WDR studio in Cologne, the cradle of German electronic music. |
| 14 | **Henry** | *Pierre Henry* (1927–2017) — Schaeffer's collaborator; *Symphonie pour un homme seul*. |
| 15 | **Ussachevsky** | *Vladimir Ussachevsky* (1911–1990) — co-founded the Columbia-Princeton Electronic Music Center. |
| 16 | **Davidovsky** | *Mario Davidovsky* (1934–2019) — *Synchronisms*: live instruments + tape, a model for human + machine. |
| 17 | **Ferrari** | *Luc Ferrari* (1929–2005) — turned field recording into composition; *Presque rien*. |
| 18 | **Lansky** | *Paul Lansky* (b. 1944) — early computer-music pioneer; Radiohead sampled his *mild und leise* for *Idioteque*. |
| 19 | **Risset** | *Jean-Claude Risset* (1938–2016) — early computer-generated music (*Mutations*); the Risset glissando illusion. |
| 20 | **Truax** | *Barry Truax* (b. 1947) — granular synthesis pioneer; *Riverrun*. |

**My top three picks from Track B:**

- **Xenakis** — closest spirit to Stockhausen (math, computers, rigorous yet free).
- **Theremin** — most iconic name; instantly evokes "music meets electronics".
- **Risset** — represents computer-generated music done with taste and rigor.

## How to choose

Two questions to answer:

1. **Do you want a name that *describes* the project (Track A)** — or a name that *honors a lineage* (Track B)?
2. **Among your favorites, say each one out loud three times.** Names that survive the third repetition are usually right.

When you pick, I'll update `NORTH_STAR.md` and `README.md` to replace *Stockhausen* everywhere.
